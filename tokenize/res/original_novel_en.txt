Title: The Lighthouse of Fog Harbor

Chapter One: Return

Xu Yan returned to Fog Harbor on the day the tide climbed over the third iron post of the old pier. The sea wind carried the smell of salt and coal smoke through the pale gray lanes, making every window rattle softly.

He had been away for seven years. Seven years earlier, he had been only the lighthouse keeper's son, leaving shore with a worn canvas bag on a cargo ship bound north. Now he was a clock repairer, coming home with an even older wooden case. Inside it were fine files, brass tweezers, springs, and three pocket watches that still refused to run.

The people of Fog Harbor had changed very little. Mrs. Lin still opened her bakery at five in the morning, the postman still liked to tuck letters into door knockers, and the black cat at the street corner still treated the fish market as its private kingdom. Only the lighthouse on the cliff had gone dark.

The lighthouse used to turn every night, its white beam sweeping across the sea like a slow and steady eye. But for the past month, it had flashed on and off without warning. When fishing boats came in, captains complained quietly in the tavern that they could no longer see the way home through the night fog.

Xu Yan's father had died three years before. Two new keepers had come and gone, and neither had managed to repair the old mechanism. The mayor knew that Xu Yan could mend clocks, so he invited him to the office and handed him a ring of rusted keys.

"Your father left things behind," the mayor said. "Perhaps you are the only one who can understand them."

Chapter Two: The Mechanism

The inside of the lighthouse was colder than Xu Yan remembered. Stone steps coiled upward, and dampness seeped through the walls. Every ten steps, the sound of waves striking the rocks seemed farther away and deeper.

The machine room was at the top. A thin layer of dust covered the great lens, and the gear assembly stood like a sleeping copper forest. Xu Yan opened his tool case, lit an oil lamp, and knelt on the floor to examine the main shaft.

The problem was not in the gears, nor in the spring. What had truly stopped the lighthouse was a tiny locating pin. Someone had bent it with force and wedged it into the groove of the drive plate. As long as it remained there, the light would stop whenever it turned due north.

Xu Yan frowned. That was not natural damage.

As he removed the pin, he heard soft footsteps behind him. He turned and saw a girl in a blue raincoat standing in the doorway, holding a stack of wet nautical charts.

"Are you Xu Yan?" she asked.

"Yes. Who are you?"

"Shen Li. My boat is the White Gull. Last week, it nearly struck North Reef at night." She entered the machine room and spread the charts on the floor. "I came to find out why."

Xu Yan handed her the bent locating pin. Shen Li looked at it, and her face darkened.

"Someone does not want the light to point north," she said.

Chapter Three: North Reef

North Reef lay three nautical miles outside Fog Harbor, with a narrow channel beneath the rocks. Old captains knew that at high tide they could use it to avoid the hidden current, but without guidance from the lighthouse, ships from elsewhere could easily be wrecked.

Shen Li said that several unfamiliar cargo ships had recently approached North Reef late at night without entering the harbor. They flew no flag, and their hulls carried no names. Whenever the lighthouse beam stopped, brief red signals appeared on the water.

Xu Yan remembered his father's diary. Back in the old house, he found the deep green notebook in the lowest drawer of the desk. On the final page was a single sentence: The North Reef channel must not be lost; when the light stops, hearts become confused.

The next night, Xu Yan and Shen Li kept watch at the top of the lighthouse. They did not repair the mechanism at once. Instead, they shaded the oil lamp and left only a narrow slit through which to watch the sea.

After midnight, the fog thickened. Three red lights suddenly flashed in the north: one short, two long. Then a black cargo ship emerged from the fog and moved quietly toward North Reef.

"They are waiting for the lighthouse to fail," Shen Li whispered.

Xu Yan fitted a replacement for the locating pin, locked the main shaft, and turned the handle. The mechanism, silent for a month, gave a low rumble. The lens rotated slowly, and a strong beam pierced the fog, sweeping straight north.

The black cargo ship was exposed in the light. A moment later, the harbor clock tower rang the alarm, and patrol boats rushed out from the pier. The cargo ship tried to turn away, but the tide pushed it toward the shallows, where it finally ran aground outside North Reef.

Chapter Four: The Light

By dawn, the fog had lifted. The patrol found smuggled kerosene, silverware, and a crate of forged harbor passes aboard the cargo ship. The person who had bent the locating pin was the former keeper. He had taken money and planned to leave Fog Harbor during the confusion.

The mayor thanked Xu Yan at the pier and asked whether he would stay to take charge of the lighthouse.

Xu Yan did not answer at once. He looked at the white tower on the cliff, then at Shen Li as she untied the White Gull's mooring line. The sea was bright again, and the distant line of waves looked like a page being turned by the wind.

"I will stay for one month," Xu Yan said. "First I will repair every part."

Shen Li smiled. "And after one month?"

Xu Yan closed his tool case. The three pocket watches inside had begun to run, their tiny ticking mixed with the sound of the tide.

"After one month," he said, "I will decide whether to sail away again."

That night, the Lighthouse of Fog Harbor lit on time. Its white beam passed over the rooftops, the pier, and North Reef, shining toward every ship on its way home.
