The Clock at the Harbor

Every evening, Mara unlocked the little clock shop after the fishermen had gone home. The shop belonged to her grandfather, who had left one unfinished repair on the bench: a brass pocket watch that refused to pass eleven fifty-nine.

One rainy night, a letter slid beneath the door. "When the harbor clock strikes twelve, bring the watch to the blue boat." Mara wound the watch once. For the first time in years, its second hand moved.

At the pier, an old sailor waited beside a boat painted the color of deep water. He took the watch gently and listened. As the town clock began its twelve slow notes, the pocket watch chimed too. The sailor smiled, looking suddenly younger, and placed a small compass in Mara's hand.

"Your grandfather promised I would find my way home," he said. The fog opened like a curtain. The blue boat sailed into the silver passage and disappeared before the final bell faded.

In the morning, Mara hung the compass above the workbench. From then on, every repaired clock left her shop ticking toward somewhere its owner needed to be.
